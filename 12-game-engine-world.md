# Chapter 12 — The Game Engine World

## Why a Game Engine Chapter

The eleven preceding chapters were organized by *representation* — Live2D's rigged sprites, ARKit's blendshapes, FLAME's PCA, the LivePortrait keypoint lineage, 3DGS clouds, diffusion latents. Game development does not fit cleanly into any one of them, because a shipped game character is not a representation; it is a *pipeline* that begins with an actor or a line of dialogue and ends with a mesh deforming on a console GPU inside a 16-millisecond frame budget. The interesting question for games is not "which representation" but "which pipeline, and which middleware fills each stage of it."

This is also the one corner of the field where the academic literature is least useful as a guide. The face-animation papers surveyed in Chapters 04–08 are overwhelmingly produced by university labs and the research arms of ByteDance, Alibaba, Kuaishou, and NVIDIA. The technology that actually animates faces in *The Last of Us Part II*, *Cyberpunk 2077*, and *Call of Duty* is a different ecosystem: a small set of specialist middleware vendors, two engine incumbents (Epic and Unity), and a tier of asset-store tooling for indie developers. These vendors publish GDC talks and SIGGRAPH *Talks* (the non-archival track), not papers, and they compete on production throughput and licensing terms rather than on FID scores. A landscape review that stops at the research front would miss the part of the field with the largest shipped install base.

This chapter surveys that ecosystem as of early 2026. It is deliberately placed after the technical chapters and the market chapter, because it is a bridge between them: game engines are where the representations of Chapters 03–08 get *consumed*, and the consumption choices are driven as much by licensing and tooling as by technical merit.

## The Two-Channel Structure of a Game Face Pipeline

Every production game-face pipeline splits into two channels that the rest of this chapter will keep referring to.

The **authoring / capture channel** runs offline, during production. A performance — an actor's face, or just a voice-over recording — is turned into animation data: blendshape weight curves, joint rotations, or rig-control values over time. This channel is allowed to be slow and expensive. It can use a head-mounted camera rig, a multi-camera scan stage, hours of solve time, and an animator's cleanup pass. Its output is baked animation assets shipped on the disc.

The **runtime channel** runs on the player's machine inside the frame budget. It evaluates the rig — converting whatever control values the authoring channel produced (or that a live system is producing) into actual vertex positions — and, increasingly, *generates* animation at runtime for content that cannot be pre-authored: barks from systemic NPCs, player-customized characters, dynamically generated dialogue, and AI-driven NPCs.

The split matters because the middleware market is segmented along exactly this line. Faceware, Dynamixyz, DI4D, and Cubic Motion's Persona are capture-channel tools. Speech Graphics' SG Com, NVIDIA Audio2Face's streaming mode, and the indie lip-sync libraries are runtime-channel tools. MetaHuman and Speech Graphics' SGX straddle both. The historical trajectory of the field is a slow migration of work from the authoring channel into the runtime channel — first lip-sync, now full emotional performance — as audio-driven models get good enough to run live. That migration is the through-line of this chapter.

## The MetaHuman Ecosystem

The single most consequential development in game-face tooling between 2021 and 2026 is Epic's MetaHuman. It is worth understanding precisely what MetaHuman *is*, because the name covers at least four distinct things.

**MetaHuman Creator** is a parametric character authoring tool: a constrained, scan-grounded face-generation interface that lets a user blend and sculpt a photorealistic human head from a library of scanned exemplars. Originally a cloud-streamed app, it was [integrated directly into Unreal Engine starting with UE 5.6](https://dev.epicgames.com/documentation/metahuman/metahuman-creator). **Mesh-to-MetaHuman** is the inverse path: it takes an arbitrary head mesh or scan and *conforms* a MetaHuman topology and rig onto it, so a studio's own scanned actor becomes a fully rigged MetaHuman. **MetaHuman Animator** is the facial-capture-and-solve system. **The MetaHuman rig itself** — the DNA file plus Rig Logic — is the runtime artifact.

MetaHuman Animator, which [left early access and shipped as a production feature in 2023](https://www.metahuman.com/news/delivering-high-quality-facial-animation-in-minutes-metahuman-animator-is-now-available) and is now folded into the engine, is the part most relevant to this review. It is multi-modal: it solves facial animation from a depth-enabled iPhone capture, from a stereo head-mounted camera, from a plain video camera or webcam, and — added later — from [audio alone](https://dev.epicgames.com/documentation/metahuman/facial-performance-capture-guidelines), producing animation with head movement and emotional shaping from just a voice track. Depth capture (an iPhone with a TrueDepth sensor, processed through the Depth Processing plugin) gives the highest fidelity; the audio-only path is the lowest-friction. The defining property is that the solve output is *semantically correct rig-control data* — it can be retargeted onto any MetaHuman with a few clicks, because every MetaHuman shares the same rig-control topology. This is the same "shared parameter space enables free retargeting" property that makes ARKit valuable (Chapter 03), applied to a much higher-dimensional and higher-fidelity rig.

The runtime artifact is the interesting engineering. A MetaHuman facial rig is a *hybrid* rig in the precise sense of Chapter 01: roughly 800 joints, more than 200 high-level expression controls, and over 1,000 corrective expression shapes, with the conversion from high-level controls to low-level joint and blendshape weights performed by **Rig Logic** — Epic's (originally 3Lateral's) rig-evaluation engine, [described in a public whitepaper](https://cdn2.unrealengine.com/rig-logic-whitepaper-v2-5c9f23f7e210.pdf). Rig Logic supports LOD slicing, so a distant NPC evaluates a cheap subset of the rig and a hero character in a cutscene evaluates the full thing. The rig and its corrective shapes are stored in a **DNA file** — a binary format that is the closest thing the games industry has to a standard rigged-head interchange format, the rough analogue of what FLAME's parameter vector is for research.

Two facts about MetaHuman matter for a 2026 reader. First, **licensing went engine-agnostic**: Epic updated MetaHuman's terms so that MetaHuman characters and animations can be exported and used in [other engines, including Unity and Godot](https://renderu.com/en/news/post/26778), not only Unreal. MetaHuman 5.7 added UV-space vertex correspondence specifically to make FBX round-tripping lossless, where earlier versions relied on fragile vertex-ordering. Second, MetaHuman's lineage *is* the lineage of two of the capture-middleware vendors discussed below: Epic acquired **3Lateral** (rig and digital-double technology) in 2019 and **Cubic Motion** (facial solving) in 2020, and MetaHuman is, in effect, those two companies' technology productized and given away to grow the Unreal ecosystem. The consolidation story — independent middleware getting absorbed into the engine — is the dominant structural fact of this chapter.

## The Live Link Face / ARKit Pipeline

Below MetaHuman Animator in cost and fidelity sits the pipeline that most small and mid-size Unreal studios actually use: **Live Link Face**, Epic's free iOS app. It is the games-industry incarnation of the ARKit standard from Chapter 03. The app runs Apple's ARKit face tracker on an iPhone, producing the [52 ARKit blendshape](https://dev.epicgames.com/documentation/unreal-engine/recording-face-animation-on-ios-device-in-unreal-engine) coefficients (plus head pose and, on TrueDepth devices, depth), and streams them over the network into Unreal via the Live Link plugin. The receiving character must expose a blendshape set that matches ARKit's 52 names, or the stream has to be retargeted with attendant loss.

Live Link Face is the cleanest example in the whole review of ARKit functioning as a *lingua franca*: a free phone app, a free engine plugin, a 52-channel standard, and any compatible character. It is not high-fidelity — 52 linear blendshapes cannot capture a subtle performance the way an 800-joint MetaHuman rig can — but it is the floor of the market, and its existence is why so much tooling in this chapter targets the ARKit-52 set as its output format. MetaHuman Animator can also *consume* the depth captured by a Live Link Face session, using it as one of its higher-quality input modes; the two are complementary rather than competitors.

## Unity's Fragmented Stack

Unity's game-face story is markedly weaker and more fragmented than Epic's, and a 2026 reader should know this before committing a project.

Unity has no MetaHuman equivalent. What it has is a scattering of partial pieces. The **Digital Human Package**, distributed under the permissive Unity Companion License, is a free library of experimental rendering features — GPU-accelerated skin deformation, wrinkle maps, skin and eye shaders — showcased in Unity's *Enemies* tech demo. It addresses *rendering* a high-fidelity face, not animating or capturing one. For capture, Unity shipped a **Live Capture** package with a companion **Unity Face Capture** iOS app, the direct analogue of Live Link Face: ARKit blendshapes streamed into the editor. But Unity has [scheduled the Live Capture package and Face Capture app for end of support](https://docs.unity3d.com/Packages/com.unity.live-capture@4.0/manual/face-capture-getting-started.html) as of Unity 6.1, and the captured animation does not cleanly export through the FBX exporter when it is driven through ARKit script components — a real workflow pothole.

The gap is filled by third parties. **Didimo** offers a cloud service that generates a rigged digital human ("a didimo") from a single selfie, with a Unity SDK and ARKit-capture integration. Reallusion's Character Creator 4 (discussed below) exports ARKit-profile characters that import into Unity. And the indie lip-sync tier — SALSA, uLipSync, the now-deprecated OVR LipSync — is overwhelmingly Unity-centric. The practical situation in 2026 is that a Unity studio assembles its face pipeline from third-party assets and its own glue code, where an Unreal studio gets a vertically integrated stack from Epic for free. This asymmetry is a genuine reason studios choose Unreal for character-heavy projects, and it compounds the consolidation dynamic: the engine that owns the face pipeline accretes the projects that need one.

## The Performance-Capture Middleware Tier

Before MetaHuman absorbed the category, AAA facial performance capture was served by a handful of specialist vendors. They still exist, and they still ship games, but the tier is visibly consolidating.

**Cubic Motion** is the cautionary tale and the origin story at once. The UK firm, founded in 2009, built **Persona** — a real-time facial-solving system that tracks an actor's face from head-mounted-camera video and streams solved animation to Unreal at 60 fps. Cubic Motion's solver, paired with **3Lateral**'s rigs, drove the landmark real-time demos of the late 2010s, including the *Hellblade: Senua's Sacrifice* live performance shown at GDC 2016 — actress Melina Juergens's face tracked from video alone, ~200 tracked facial features, solved and rendered as Senua in roughly 16 milliseconds, a feat that won a SIGGRAPH Real-Time Live award. The technology was used on *God of War* and *Marvel's Spider-Man*. Then Epic acquired Cubic Motion in March 2020, the same way it had acquired 3Lateral the year before, and the combined technology became MetaHuman. Persona as an independently licensable product effectively ceased to exist; its DNA is now inside the engine.

**Faceware Technologies** is the survivor of the markerless-video-capture category. Faceware Studio performs real-time markerless facial tracking and solving from ordinary video, and the company sells head-mounted camera hardware alongside it. Faceware was itself acquired — by Facebank Group in 2021 — but continues to operate as a distinct product line, and Faceware Studio remains a production-ready, separately licensable option in 2026 for studios that do not want to commit to MetaHuman's topology. **Dynamixyz**, the French markerless-capture firm, was Faceware's main competitor in that niche; it was acquired by Take-Two Interactive in 2021 and is no longer the independent force it was, leaving Faceware as the principal standalone vendor.

**DI4D** occupies the high-fidelity end. Rather than solving a rig from sparse markers, DI4D's **PURE4D** pipeline captures dense *4D* data — a per-frame reconstructed mesh sequence of the actor's face — and retargets that dense performance onto the game rig, preserving detail that a sparse solve discards. DI4D delivered the [facial animation for *Call of Duty: Modern Warfare II*](https://www.gdcvault.com/play/1029448/The-Facial-Animation-Pipeline-of), processing over four hours of facial performance for cinematics and in-game animation, after having done 25 minutes of cinematics for the 2019 *Modern Warfare*. DI4D is a service-and-pipeline vendor more than a shrink-wrapped product: studios engage it for a project rather than licensing a tool.

The pattern across this tier: the capture *category* is healthy and still ships the biggest games, but the *independent vendor count* is shrinking. The most capable performance-capture technology in the industry is now either inside Unreal (the ex-Cubic-Motion, ex-3Lateral stack) or sold as a bespoke service (DI4D). The shrink-wrapped, license-it-yourself middle has thinned to roughly Faceware.

## The Audio-Driven Middleware Tier

The audio-driven tier is where the field is moving, because it attacks the cost that dominates a modern game's face budget: a 100-hour RPG with full voice acting in a dozen languages cannot hand-animate, or even motion-capture, every line. Audio-driven middleware turns the voice recording itself into facial animation. Three vendors matter.

**JALI Research**, spun out of academic work at the University of Toronto and Simon Fraser University, is the procedural-animation incumbent. The JALI model — introduced in the 2016 SIGGRAPH paper *JALI: an animator-centric viseme model for expressive lip synchronization* — decomposes mouth motion onto two intuitive axes, **JA** (jaw) and **LI** (lip), and layers procedurally generated paralinguistic motion (brows, eyes, head, neck) on top, all driven by independent per-language linguistic models. Its breakout production use was *Cyberpunk 2077*: CD Projekt Red used JALI to procedurally animate the faces of *every speaking character in the open world*, lip-synced across ten languages, with no facial motion capture for the bulk of the cast — a job described in the SIGGRAPH 2020 *Talk* "JALI-Driven Expressive Facial Animation and Multilingual Speech in Cyberpunk 2077." JALI ships as **JALI 2.0** today, integrating with Maya (2020–2025) and Unreal Engine (5.0–5.6) via a command-line interface, supporting roughly a dozen languages, with publicly associated clients including CD Projekt Red, Activision, Riot Games, and Blizzard. It remains a proprietary, studio-licensed tool; pricing is negotiated. JALI's recent public activity has been the cross-vendor *Anim Revolution* pipeline collective, presented at GDC 2025.

**Speech Graphics**, an Edinburgh firm, is JALI's principal rival and arguably the broader-deployed of the two. Its technology is audio-driven facial *dynamics*: it analyzes voice audio and procedurally simulates the muscle activity that produced it, generating not just lip-sync but emotion, breath, and laughter. The product line splits cleanly along this chapter's two channels — **SGX** is the offline authoring tool (audio file in, animation file out, after a one-time per-character setup file), and **SG Com** is the real-time runtime version for live or dynamically generated speech. Speech Graphics claims an extremely broad AAA footprint — its [own materials cite ~90% of AAA publishers](https://80.lv/articles/a-deep-dive-into-speech-graphics-facial-animation-solutions) and games including *The Last of Us Part II*, *Hogwarts Legacy*, *Resident Evil Village*, *Gotham Knights*, and *Dying Light 2*. Its 2024–2025 development has pushed into nonverbal animation (audio-driven breath and laughter) and an expanded Unreal integration, positioning SG Com squarely for the runtime, AI-NPC use case.

**NVIDIA Audio2Face** is the disruptor, and it is disruptive specifically because of its licensing. Audio2Face-3D is a deep model (regression and diffusion variants exist) that generates 3D facial animation from audio — jaw, tongue, eyes, and skin articulation — and can run offline or as a real-time stream. It outputs blendshape curves, joint transforms, or direct mesh deformation, and ships with an [ACE Unreal plugin](https://docs.nvidia.com/ace/ace-unreal-plugin/2.5/ace-unreal-plugin-animation.html) that includes a sample mapping onto MetaHuman blendshapes. In September 2025 NVIDIA [open-sourced Audio2Face](https://developer.nvidia.com/blog/nvidia-open-sources-audio2face-animation-model/): the SDK, plugins, and training framework went to GitHub under MIT and Apache licenses, with model weights under the NVIDIA Open Model License. This is a categorical break from JALI and Speech Graphics, both of which are closed, negotiated, proprietary tools — a free, open, retrainable audio-to-face model changes the economics of the entire tier. There is a real caveat: NVIDIA's *service-specific terms* restrict production use of the *hosted* Audio2Face microservice to evaluation only, so a studio shipping it must integrate the local open-source SDK, not depend on NVIDIA's cloud. Documented production users include Farm51 (*Chernobylite 2*), Survios (*Alien: Rogue Incursion*), and Krafton (*inZOI*).

The trajectory is unambiguous. JALI represents the procedural-linguistics generation; Speech Graphics the procedural-muscle-simulation generation; Audio2Face the learned-model generation. Each is more automated and less hand-tuned than the last, and the newest one is open-source. The audio-driven channel is on the same path the rest of the field is on — from hand-authored to statistical to learned — and games are where it has the most immediate commercial pull, because games have the most dialogue to animate.

## The Indie Lip-Sync Tier

Below the AAA middleware sits a tier of inexpensive or free lip-sync tooling that serves the vastly larger population of indie and mid-size developers. It is worth cataloguing because it is where most *games*, by count, get their face animation.

**Oculus / Meta OVR LipSync** was for years the default free option: a Unity and Unreal plugin mapping audio to **15 language-agnostic visemes** (`sil, PP, FF, TH, DD, kk, CH, SS, nn, RR, aa, E, ih, oh, ou`), each driving a morph target. The 15-viseme set is itself a minor standard, distinct from ARKit's 52 blendshapes and from Disney's classic mouth-shape sets. OVR LipSync is now **end-of-life** — Meta froze it and points developers to the Movement SDK, which delivers the same 15 visemes through the `XR_META_face_tracking_visemes` OpenXR extension. Its long deprecation tail means it is still in countless shipped projects.

**Rhubarb Lip Sync** (already noted in Chapters 02 and 06) is the open-source offline workhorse: a command-line tool that analyzes a recording and emits a track of a small set of mouth shapes, used for hand-rigged 2D, Live2D, and low-budget 3D. **SALSA LipSync Suite** is the dominant commercial Unity asset (~$39 on the Asset Store): notably, it is *amplitude-based and language-agnostic* — it approximates lip motion from the audio envelope rather than doing phoneme recognition, trading accuracy for zero per-line setup, and it animates blendshapes, transforms, or sprites, so it serves stylized and 2D characters as readily as 3D ones. **uLipSync** is the open-source alternative: an MFCC-based Unity plugin built on the Job System and Burst compiler, comparing live audio against pre-registered phoneme parameters to drive a SkinnedMeshRenderer's blendshapes.

**Reallusion's Character Creator 4 and iClone** straddle the indie and mid-tier markets and deserve a specific mention for how they bridge to the standards in this review. CC4 native characters carry 140 blendshapes derived from 4D scanning, and Reallusion ships an explicit ARKit-aligned facial profile: the **ExPlus / AccuLips** profile combines the 52 ARKit blendshapes with a set of additional tongue and corrective shapes (CC4 reports 63 custom blendshapes in total). **AccuLips** is Reallusion's audio- or text-driven lip-sync, extracting visemes with co-articulation modeling. CC4 is, in effect, an affordable, ARKit-compatible authoring environment that feeds both Unity and Unreal — the budget counterpart to MetaHuman Creator.

## Runtime Representations and the Bridges to the Rest of This Review

Pulling the survey together: what representation does a face actually live in once it is inside a game engine, and how does that connect to Chapters 03–08?

The runtime representation in games is almost always one of two things, usually both at once. **Blendshapes / morph targets** are the universal currency — every engine imports them via FBX, the ARKit-52 and OVR-15 sets are blendshape sets, and every audio-driven tool in this chapter can emit blendshape curves. **Joint / bone rigs** are the other half: skin deformation, jaw rotation, and eye aim are typically done with joints, and high-end rigs like MetaHuman's are joint-dominant (800 joints) with blendshapes used as correctives. The conversion from artist-facing controls to these low-level deformers is **rig logic** — Epic's Rig Logic is the named, documented instance, but every studio rig has an equivalent layer. **FACS** (the Facial Action Coding System) is the conceptual vocabulary behind most of these rigs: hero rigs are frequently organized as FACS action units, and ARKit's 52 blendshapes are a FACS-influenced selection. **Visemes** are the speech-specific subset: the 15 OVR visemes, the JALI viseme model, AccuLips visemes.

The load-bearing observation for this review is what is *absent*. **FLAME — the spine of Chapters 04, 07, and much of 08 — barely appears in shipped games.** FLAME is a research interchange model; games use ARKit-shaped blendshapes and bespoke joint rigs. A pipeline that produces FLAME parameters (DECA, EMOCA, SMIRK, the FLAME-rigged 3DGS avatars of Chapter 07, the FLAME-conditioned diffusion of Chapter 08) cannot drop its output into a game without crossing a bridge — and that bridge is exactly the ARKit↔FLAME conversion analyzed in Chapter 09, the same conversion the FLAME chapter and the `vamp-interface` research notes treat as a first-class problem. The game-engine world and the research world meet at the 52-channel ARKit blendshape interface and almost nowhere else. Conversely, the audio-driven talking-head models of Chapter 06 (Audio2Face, EmoTalk, DiffPoseTalk, UniTalker) are *directly* relevant here, because several of them already emit ARKit-52 or FLAME-expression coefficients — Audio2Face is, after all, simultaneously a Chapter 06 talking-head model and a Chapter 12 game middleware product. That double membership is not an accident of taxonomy; it is the field's two halves converging on the same runtime channel.

## What the Game Engine World Tells the Rest of the Review

Three conclusions carry forward.

First, **consolidation around the engine is the structural story.** The independent facial-middleware market that existed in 2018 — Cubic Motion, 3Lateral, Faceware, Dynamixyz as four separate licensable vendors — has by 2026 collapsed into "inside Unreal" (the first two), "the surviving standalone" (Faceware), and "bespoke service" (DI4D). Epic gives away, as MetaHuman, what used to be a six-figure middleware stack, because owning the face pipeline accretes the projects. Unity's comparative weakness here is a real competitive liability.

Second, **the migration from the authoring channel to the runtime channel is accelerating, and audio is the vector.** Hand-keyed animation gave way to performance capture (authoring channel); performance capture is now giving way, for the long tail of dialogue, to audio-driven generation that can run live (runtime channel). JALI → Speech Graphics → Audio2Face is that progression compressed into three vendors, and the newest of the three being open-source means the cost of entry to high-quality audio-driven face animation just dropped to roughly zero. The AI-NPC use case — characters whose dialogue is generated at runtime and therefore *cannot* be pre-animated — makes the runtime audio-driven channel not merely cheaper but mandatory.

Third, **games and research meet at ARKit and nowhere else.** The blendshape interface — concretely, the ARKit-52 set — is the one representation that the game-engine world and the academic FLAME/diffusion world both speak. FLAME does not ship in games; diffusion latents do not ship in games; LivePortrait keypoints do not ship in games. Any technology from Chapters 04–08 that wants to reach a shipped game character has to be converted to blendshapes first, which is precisely why Chapter 09's bridge analysis, and the ARKit-versus-FLAME decision in Chapter 03, are load-bearing rather than academic. The game engine is where this review's representations go to be consumed, and the customs checkpoint at the border is 52 blendshapes wide.
